# Philosophy

## The Insight

You're not learning a new framework. You're applying what you already know about running teams.

The patterns that made your human teams effective — context handoffs, role specialization, phase-appropriate discipline — work with AI for the same reasons they worked with humans.

AI agents need onboarding. They need clear scope. They need handoffs that capture "why," not just "what." They need different levels of autonomy for different types of decisions.

These aren't AI-specific requirements. They're coordination requirements. The substrate changed. The patterns stay.

---

## What This Means

**If you've built companies before:**
- You already know when to checkpoint vs. when to trust
- You already know why handoffs need reasoning, not just facts
- You already know that context is expensive to rebuild
- You already know that the last two weeks before launch are different

This repository encodes that intuition into a form AI can follow.

**If you haven't:**
- You're learning how to run a team, not how to use a tool
- The skills transfer to human teams
- The patterns are battle-tested across multiple companies

---

## The Difference

Most people treat AI as a tool. Configure it, prompt it, get output.

This treats AI as a team member. Onboard it, give it context, establish operating agreements. The investment in structure pays off the same way it does with humans — in reduced coordination overhead and fewer dropped balls.

---

## What AI Does Well

| Advantage | What It Means |
|-----------|---------------|
| **No context decay** | Files don't forget. Monday's handoff is still there Friday. |
| **Perfect parallelization** | Multiple agents, no meetings, no Slack overhead |
| **No ego in role transitions** | Backend → Frontend → QA without friction |
| **Scales to attention** | Your judgment is the bottleneck, not headcount |

---

## What AI Does Poorly

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|------------|
| **No pushback on bad ideas** | "When uncertain, ask" rule. Explicit checkpoints. Periodically ask "is there a better approach?" |
| **No ambient awareness** | Document assumptions. Ask clarifying questions. AI doesn't see what you don't tell it. |
| **No creative disagreement** | You have to prompt for alternatives. AI won't volunteer "actually, we should rethink this." |
| **Will "improve" things unhelpfully** | Phase discipline. Pre-launch mode. Explicit boundaries on scope. |

These risks are real. The mitigations are explicit in the methodology.

---

## The Founder's Role

You're not delegating to a black box. You're running a team.

- You set direction (same as always)
- You make judgment calls (same as always)
- You create the structure that lets others work effectively (same as always)
- You code when your judgment is needed in the details (same as always)

The team just happens to be AI.

---

## Why Explicit Structure Matters More

With humans, much coordination happens through culture and osmosis. People pick up norms by watching. They learn what you care about through repeated interaction. They remember last week's conversation.

AI doesn't have this. Every session starts fresh. What was implicit with humans must be explicit with AI:

| With Humans | With AI |
|-------------|---------|
| Culture communicates standards | `_CONVENTIONS.md` documents standards |
| Memory carries context | `_AGENTS.md` carries context |
| Seniority implies decision rights | `decisions.md` specifies decision rights |
| Experience brings phase discipline | `_PRELAUNCH.md` enforces phase discipline |

The structure isn't bureaucracy. It's the translation layer between your intuition and the new substrate.

---

## The Payoff

When it works, you have:

- Multiple workstreams progressing in parallel
- Context that survives across sessions
- Handoffs that capture reasoning
- Phase-appropriate discipline without constant reminders
- Your judgment applied where it matters, not on every detail

This is what a well-run team feels like. The substrate changed. The feeling is the same.
